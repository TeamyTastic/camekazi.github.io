Key reframe

Friction Arbitrage

"Make traditional development incrementally harder. Make mobbing incrementally easier. Wait."

You're not changing minds. You're changing the cost-benefit landscape. Then you step back and watch what people choose.

1 Reduce Friction for Mobbing ‚ñº

Don't change how all development happens. Just make it stupid easy to mob when someone wants to.

  • Standing mob slot: Block Tue/Thu 10-12 as "mob-available time"
  • Default mob space: One room permanently configured
  • Remote mob kit: Pre-configured Zoom + VS Code Live Share
  • Role cards: Navigator / Driver / Observer printed
  • Rotation timer: Makes switching a ritual, not negotiation
2 Make Solo Work Costs Visible ‚ñº

You're not blocking solo work‚Äîyou're making its hidden costs visible.

  • Stuck protocol: "Blocked >90 min? Drop it in #mob-room"
  • Track PR turnaround: Time from open to merge
  • Bus factor matrix: Component √ó Who understands it
  • Shift standup: "What moved forward?" not "What did you do?"
3 Generate Comparison Data ‚ñº

Present without editorializing: "Here's what we're seeing..."

  • Cycle time: Idea ‚Üí Production (include all rework)
  • Knowledge spread: Survey understanding after 4 weeks
  • Stuck-work metric: Time between stuck and unstuck

The Trojan Mice Approach

Trojan Mice: Small, seemingly innocuous changes that slip past organizational antibodies and create bigger shifts over time. Don't call it "adopting mob programming"‚Äîthat triggers immune responses.

"We keep finding bugs in production"
"Want to try mobbing on the risky stuff? Built-in review."
"PRs sit for days waiting for review"
"What if we just... didn't have PRs for this? Mob it?"
"Only one person understands the auth system"
"Let's mob a feature in auth so everyone learns it."
"This integration is gnarly"
"Sounds like a good mob candidate‚Äîshare the pain."

"You're not asking permission to change development culture. You're proposing a tactical solution to a specific problem."

Key Moves

üéØ Tactical Move: Don't Ask Permission

Don't say "implement mob programming." Say you're "reserving some collaboration time" and set it up.

üõ°Ô∏è Risk Removal: Shoulder It Yourself

Put your name on the experiment. Track and share metrics yourself. Tell your manager you're trying this‚Äîask for 2 weeks.

üéØ Target Selection: High-Value Stuck Work

Don't mob on easy tasks (looks wasteful). Mob on: "We've been stuck 3 days" or "This is critical and risky."

Designing a Safe-to-Fail Experiment

"The goal is discovery of something unknown and the compounding of learning‚Äînot proving you were right."

The Ready

Treat the organization as a "weather system" not a "watch." Outcomes are unpredictable‚Äîthe only way to understand is to poke the system and see how it reacts.

The Hypothesis Format

"We believe that [action] will result in [outcome] because [reason]"

For mob programming:

"We believe that making mob slots available for stuck work will result in faster unblocking and broader knowledge spread because real-time collaboration eliminates handoff delays and creates shared context."

Safe-to-Try Tests

Before launching, the experiment must pass these hurdles:

1 The Waterline Test ‚ñº

Above the waterline: Safe to try, easy to patch if it leaks

Below the waterline: Could sink the ship‚Äîneeds explicit consent

üö¢ Mob programming is above the waterline

Worst case: A few wasted calendar hours. No production risk, no customer impact, fully reversible.

2 The Blast Radius Test ‚ñº

Run in a small, constrained context to limit potential negative consequences.

  • Limited to one team (not company-wide rollout)
  • Opt-in participation (no mandates)
  • Fixed duration (8 weeks max before review)
3 The Adjacent Possible Test ‚ñº

Target the state "right next door"‚Äînot an insurmountable leap.

‚ùå Too big "All development will now be mob programming"
‚úÖ Adjacent "Mob slots available when someone's stuck"
4 The Authority Test ‚ñº

Do you have the explicit right to change ways of working in this domain?

  • Manager aware and supportive (even if not championing)
  • Team has autonomy over their collaboration methods
  • Not treated as "side-of-desk"‚Äîreal time allocated

Common Failure Modes

Experiments fail due to design flaws, not bad ideas:

Excessive Complexity
Testing too many variables at once. Focus on ONE change.
Insufficient Boldness
So safe it can't generate meaningful learning. Be radical at non-radical scale.
Organizational Antibodies
Killed before it has time to yield data. Protect the experiment explicitly.
Premature Judgement
Declaring success/failure before timeline elapsed. Commit to the duration.

Kill / Pivot / Scale Decision

Signal Action
>15% improvement + positive feedback + low resource cost Scale
Goal valid but approach ineffective‚Äînew insights available Pivot
No meaningful impact, high cost, or abandoned for 6 months Kill

"In a healthy experimental culture, most experiments should fail. A 90% success rate means you weren't being bold enough to learn."

The 8-Week Experiment

üß† Why 8 Weeks?

The Dip: Weeks 1-2 capture only the "hell" of transition‚Äîthe discomfort of unlearning. The good stuff is on the other side.

Conscious Incompetence: At 2-3 weeks, teams think they've tried it and want to quit. They haven't built the muscle yet.

Different Learning Paces: Not everyone arrives at insights at the same speed. 8 weeks ensures the whole group finds the value.

Weeks 1-2: The Dip

This will feel harder, not easier. That's normal‚Äînew muscles.

  • Mob slots available when stuck or high-risk
  • Expect awkwardness, coordination friction
  • Don't judge yet‚Äîyou're in conscious incompetence

Track: What got mobbed, why, early observations

Weeks 3-4: The Messy Middle

Sunk cost bias kicks in. Resist the urge to start a new experiment.

  • Pick ONE feature to mob start-to-finish
  • Search for signal in the noise
  • First "oh shit, that was fast" moments appear

Checkpoint: Quick retro‚Äîadjust format, don't abandon

Weeks 5-6: Building Muscle

The practice starts to feel natural. Patterns emerge.

  • Team develops its own mob rhythm
  • Knowledge spread becomes visible
  • Comparison data accumulates

Track: Cycle time, bugs, knowledge surveys

Weeks 7-8: The Summit

Now you can objectively assess. Full 8-week data set.

  • What made mobbing hard? (Be honest)
  • What made it valuable?
  • Decide: Scale, Pivot, or Kill

"At 2-3 weeks, teams think they've tried it. They haven't. They've only experienced conscious incompetence‚Äîthe discomfort of not yet having the muscle."

What Needs to Be True

Diagnostic checklist for each phase of the flow:

üéØ Context: Identifying the Problem ‚ñº
  • Problem is genuinely stuck (not routine work)
  • Mob agrees it's worth the overhead
  • Someone can articulate why it's stuck
üß† Understanding: Building Shared Context ‚ñº
  • Navigator has clarity on the goal, not just the task
  • Constraints are nameable
  • Mob can ask "dumb questions" safely
  • AI has codebase access
  • Mob can evaluate if AI context is relevant (not just accept it)
  • Everyone sees the same screen
  • Time allocated to actually understand, not just nod
‚å®Ô∏è Implementation: Building Together ‚ñº
  • Navigator has a hypothesis (can be wrong)
  • Driver can translate direction ‚Üí action
  • Mob can challenge the approach
  • AI options are reviewable chunks (not walls of code)
  • Mob can compare/evaluate, not just accept first suggestion
  • Driver understands what they're typing
  • Mechanism exists for "wait, stop"
üîç Review: Validating Together ‚ñº
  • Tests exist
  • Feedback is fast (<30 sec)
  • Failures visible to everyone, not just driver
  • Time to actually look (not just rubber-stamp)
  • Diverse perspectives engaged
  • "LGTM" is interrogated
üöÄ Integration: Shipping It ‚ñº
  • Clear definition of "done"
  • Navigator isn't a bottleneck
  • Mob can override if consensus disagrees
  • Commit message captures shared understanding
  • Mob gets credit (not just driver's name)
