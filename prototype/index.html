Key Finding

Volume per session, not volume per week,
predicts marathon outcome.

The 2019 build had the highest session frequency (13.2/week) but averaged only 7.9 km per run — and produced the slowest time by 3+ minutes. Every build that broke 2:20 averaged 11.3 km or more per session.

The target for the next build: maintain 11–13 sessions/week but protect average session length at 12 km+. Better to cut a session entirely than run a junk 5 km.

What Was Consistent Across All 5 Builds
01 / Constant
Race-length long runs
Every build's longest single run landed between 39.3–42.8 km. No exceptions. This is not conventional — it's aggressive and apparently non-negotiable for Ewan.
02 / Constant
Double-digit weekly sessions
All 5 builds averaged 11+ sessions/week. Even the leanest (Valencia 2023 at 11.2/week) never dropped below 10 in any single training week.
03 / Constant
Hard taper, 3 weeks out
Volume drops sharply and consistently in the final 3 weeks across all 5 builds, with the steepest cut in the final 7 days. The mechanism varies; the pattern doesn't.

Coaching Verdict

The aerobic engine is there.
The question is what a realistic next build looks like.

Average HR across the build blocks tells an encouraging story. The two fastest builds sit at 121–128 bpm average. The COVID volume surge (2020–21) genuinely built the aerobic base — it wasn't junk miles. That base hasn't decayed dramatically in the HR data.

Valencia 2023 equalled Valencia 2021's time on 221 km less volume, which suggests improved running economy (RE 0.981 vs 0.956 at Goodwood). The next breakthrough is more likely to come from race-day execution and power output than from adding more kilometres.

The Goodwood 2020 power output of 5.31 W/kg has never been matched since. That's the target — not 2,300 km of build volume, but arriving at the start line with that engine primed.
